Tracking sensors with picosecond timing resolution are a relatively recent development for detectors in high energy physics. The ATLAS detector will have partial timing coverage with the Phase-2 ugparde \cite{hgtd-tdr}, while the CMS detector will have almost full coverage \cite{cms-mtd-tdr}. This is an important development to tackle the increasing number of simultaneous proton-proton interactions from an average of 54 in Run-3 to about 140 in Run-4 (HL-LHC pile-up challenge). During reconstruction, this new measurement dimension can be used for pile-up rejection, particle identification and flavor tagging to improve physics performance. At the same time it can help to reduce the combinatorial complexity of the event and therefore reduce the computational cost to reconstruct it.

The time dimension needs to be processed not only in hardward but also software. Additional quantities need to be stored in the EDM and processed by reconstruction algorithms. This is a challenge for existing tracking software, as algorithms need to be re-evaluated if time information can be used, upgraded and validated.

To study the impact of the additional time measurements on tracking and physics performance, assumtions have to be made about the time resolution of the sensors and the amount of material in the detector. In the best case, these studies involve detailed simulations of the detector and reconstruction with algorithms that fully utilize this new dimension. In reality, assumptions cannot be avoided if the detector has not been designed yet and full reconstruction chains have not been developed or properly optimized. New algorithms utilizing time information have to be developed, tested and optimized which consumes time and resources. As an alternative, further assumptions can be made about the performance of these new algorithms to obtain early physics results. This was done in a recent ATLAS study which assumes a detector upgrade after Run-4 with almost full time coverage \cite{pub-2023-023}: It investiages the potential of 4D tracking for vertexing and flavor tagging, and then ultimately for some selected physics analyses.

This chapter describes the developments in Acts to further support and improve 4D tracking. It is a summary of the work done during this thesis and the current state of the software. The chapter is structured as follows: first, existing 4D tracking capabilities in Acts are presented and correlations with the time parameter are discussed. Then, changes to the individual components of the tracking pipeline are described. This includes the propagation of time information from clusters to spacepoints, seeding with time information, track finding and fitting with time information, and vertex finding and fitting with time information. The chapter concludes with a summary of the current state and an outlook on future developments.

\section{4D tracking in Acts}

4D tracking is a long standing capability in Acts. Time quantities can be stored in almost all EDMs, including measurements, spacepoints, track parameters, track states, tracks themselves, and vertices. As such, all the algorithms connected to these EDMs can make use of time information, but not all of them are adapted to do so. Cluster cells and seeds with time information have been explored to some degree but not fully implemented yet.

If measurements carry time information, it can be accessed in the same way as other measurement dimensions. Time is also automatically transported in the propagation and can be picked up by algorithms that rely on it. Examples are the track finding and fitting algorithms which depend on predicted track parameters to compare them with measurements and compute an update. For these kinds of algorithms the use of time information comes practically for free. If $\chi^2$ cuts are used, the user only needs to be aware that the thresholds need to be adjusted in order to keep the p-value similar to the case without time information.

While some algorithms do not need special treatment to utilize time information, others need to be adapted and configured correctly to make use of it. At the moment, the track EDM does not make a difference if parameters are fitted or not. For this reason, tracks with and without time information can only be distinguished by looking into the measurement content or taking a decision based on the variance of the time parameter. The variance will be either on the order of the resolution of the time measurements or the initial variance of the parameter. This is a limitation of the current EDM implementation which should be addressed in the future.

Acts does not support clustering with time information yet. This could be a useful feature to split clusters based on time information. Additionally, the time resolution could be improved by combining individual cell data. This is currently under investigation and will be implemented in the future.

\section{Correlations with the time parameter}

Generally it is not expected that time information will have an impact on the spatial resolution of tracks and vertices during the fit. This is due to the different scales of spatial and temporal measurement resolutions, and the scales at which experiments are built. Spatial measurements are on the order of 10 μm while temporal measurements are on the order of 10 ps which correspords to about 3 mm. Given these resolutions and usual experiment dimensions, the correlations between spatial and time measurements are negligible and therefore have virtually no impact on the fit. This can be seen in the correlation matrix of the bound track parameters at the interaction point with single muons in the OpenDataDetector. \section{Spacepoints with time}

After clusters and measurements with time information have been formed, the information can be further propagated into spacepoints. Spacepoints are a basic building block for pattern recognition algortihms. Additional information on spacepoints can be used to cut combinatorics in the seeding stage.

During the work on this thesis, spacepoints were extended to carry time information if available. This was a necessary step to further propagate this information to the track parameter estimation for seeds. The track finding will then automatically pick up this initial time estimate.

\section{Seeding with time}

Seeding is the first step in the track finding process. It is responsible for creating track candidates from spacepoints. This is a combinatorial problem and cuts are used to reduce the number of candidates. Time information can be used to cut the combinatorics further down, therefore reducing the computational cost. This requires having at least two spacepoints with time information, or just one and an estimate of the collision time to cut spacepoints produced by previous events.

The current triplet seeding implementation in Acts does not make use of time information. However, there has been an early study to estimate the potential of using time information during seeding \cite{bos_2023_h2cbz-as705}. The results are promising and show a reduction in the number of fakes and computational cost. Using time information during seeding is one of the action items for the future.

During the work on this thesis time information has been included in the parameter estimation of triplet seeds. This is a necessary step to further propagate this information to the track finding. It is a very simple implementation for now that just picks up the time information from the bottom spacepoint if available. This could be further improved by taking the time information from any spacepoint, extrapolate it to the bottom spacepoint and combine it to form a single, improved estimate.

\section{Track finding and fitting with time}

In Acts, track finding and fitting algorithms automatically use time information if it is provided by the measurements. This comes practically for free as both rely heavily on the propagation which transports time unconditionally.

The track finding will automatically select and cut on the additional parameter based on the predicition of the track parameters on the sensor. The standard cutting mechanism in Acts is a $\chi^2$ cut which extends automatically to the time parameter. Users only need to be aware that the $\chi^2$ cut parameter might need to be adjusted to keep the p-value similar to the case without time information.

Same is true for the track fitting. Fitting algorithms in Acts are generic and do not need adaptation to work with time information. The time information is automatically picked up from the measurements and used for the fit.

In both cases, the initial parameters play a crucial role in the performance of the algorithms. The variance of the time parameter needs to be properly inflated to reflect our initial uncertainty and to avoid biasing the fit.

\section{Primary vertex finding with time}

Vertex finding is the process of grouping tracks into vertices. This is generally a combinatorial problem but in practice a seeding step is used to identify vertex candidates and tracks are assigned to them based on their compatibility. In case of primary vertex finding, the search can be constrained to the beam line. Since only a single dimension is left, tracks that originate from close-by interactions cannot be distinguished and will be merged into a single vertex. Time information provides an additional dimension to group tracks to vertices and to discriminate vertices close in space but separated in time. This can reduce undesired effects, e.g. vertex merging and splitting, and improve physics performance. Figure \ref{fig:dev/4d-tracking/grid-density} shows the track density of a simulated \ttbar event at $\langle \mu \rangle = 200$. The separation of vertices is clearly visible with the additional time dimension. The performance of vertex finding with time information using Acts is currently under investigation and parts of the work will be presented in Chapter \ref{ch:perf/odd}.

Current vertex seeders in Acts use a gaussian track density to estimate vertex candidates. It is implemented in two flavors: using an analytical representation to find the maximum of the density, and using a discrete numerical evaluated grid. During the work on this thesis, in collaboration with another student, the grid based seeder has been adapted to make use of time information. Effectively, the 1D grid was extended to 2D, to include time information, and then optimized for sparsity to reduce the compute time and memory consumption. The analytical seeder is not adapted for time yet.

Additionally to the adapted grid seeder, a truth seeder has been implemented to study the vertexing performance in presence of optimal seeding. The truth seeder uses the true vertex position, and optionally time, to create seeds for the finder. This is a useful tool to study the impact of the seeder on the vertex finding performance. During validation it was observed that the order of the seeds has an impact on the finder performance. For this reason, truth vertices are sorted by the number of tracks before they are passed to the seeder to express their priority.

Since the track EDM does not distinguish between tracks with and without time information, the vertex seeders and finders have a switch to use time information or not. The implementation is then specialized in different places using this switch. For vertex finders this affects the compatibility checks of tracks with vertices and the track assignment to vertices. In case of some tracks having time information and some not, the time covariance should be used to reflect this. Similar to the track finding, the $\chi^2$ thresholds have to be adjusted to keep the p-value similar to the case without time information.

\section{Vertex fitting with time}

Vertex fitting is the process of estimating the vertex position and its covariance from the assigned tracks. At the same time, track parameters and their covariances can be refined. Acts has implementations for a Billoir \cite{billoir} and a Kalman vertex fitter \cite{fruehwrith}. Both have been adapted to include time information. This was done by another student while a supporting role was provided during the work on this thesis. A detailed description is expected to be published in the near future. Adding support for time information required changes in the track linearization which is used by the fitters to update vertex and track parameters. Similar to the vertex seeders and finders, the vertex fitters have a switch to use time information or not.

During the validation of the vertex fitters, a bias in the fitter's time estimate has been observed. This was believed to be due to an incorrect particle hypothesis being used during the fit. Usually, the particle hypothesis is set to pions, which is a reasonable assumption for hadron colliders, but not valid for all produced particles. Indeed, using a truth based particle hypothesis removed the bias. CMS has studied estimating the particle hypothesis from the time information during the fit \cite{cms-time-vertexing}. Acts might follow a similiar approach in the future.

The performance of vertex fitting with time information is currently under investigation and part of the work will be presented in Chapter \ref{ch:perf/odd}.

\section{Summary and outlook}

4D tracking in Acts has matured over the course of this thesis. Time information can be passed around via almost all EDMs. It is automatically picked up by track finding and fitting algorithms. Vertex finders and fitters have been adapted to include time information. The performance of the algorithms has been studied to some degree and show promising results.

Nevertheless, there are still some areas that need further development.
\begin{itemize}
    \item Clustering with time information is not supported yet. This could be a useful feature to split clusters based on time information and the time resolution might be improved by combining individual cell data.
    \item Seeding with time information has been explored but not integrated into Acts.
    \item Estimating the particle hypothesis during vertex fitting with time information is a promising approach for particle identifications and removing potential biases. This is currently not supported in Acts but might be added in the future.
\end{itemize}

4D tracking is a powerful tool to improve the physics performance of detectors in high energy physics. It is a challenging task to develop and optimize the algorithms to make use of this new measurement dimension. Acts is a versatile reconstruction toolkit that can be used to study the impact of time information on tracking and physics performance. The work done during this thesis was one step further in this direction.
