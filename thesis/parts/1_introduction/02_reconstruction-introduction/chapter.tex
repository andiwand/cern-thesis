Reconstruction is the process of extracting underlying physical information from the raw data collected by a detector. Due to the complexity of the process, reconstruction is usually done in a series of steps. Each step takes the output of the previous step and produces new results. This procedure can be thought of as building increasingly higher-level objects in a chain of reconstruction steps.

This thesis is mainly concerned with the reconstruction of tracks from charged particles produced by primary interactions and the reconstruction of their vertices. Track reconstruction is a crucial step in the reconstruction of the full event in a particle physics experiment. It is the process of reconstructing the trajectories of charged particles after they traversed the detector. After a trajectory is found and fitted to the detector measurements, the track can be used to estimate the particle's momentum, charge, and origin.

In a subsequent step called vertex reconstruction, tracks are used to find the points where the particles were produced, called vertices. The position of the vertices in comparison to the interaction region can be used to gain insight into the event processes.

For the reconstruction of the full event, tracks and vertices are used to form higher-level objects like jets, missing transverse energy (MET), taus, and others.

Displaced tracks and vertices are not covered in this thesis. They can be created by interactions with the detector material or decays of long-lived particles. Displaced tracks and vertices are usually reconstructed in a separate step after the standard reconstruction, also called large radius tracking (LRT).

This chapter introduces the key concepts and methods involved in track and vertex reconstruction. It begins with an overview of the relevant particle-matter interactions and the equations of motion for charged particles in a magnetic field. Then, the Runge-Kutta-Nyström method is presented as the numerical technique used to integrate these equations. Subsequently, the concept of track parameters is discussed, along with the transport of Jacobians and covariance matrices. Finally, the reconstruction chains and objects for tracks and vertices are described, along with the various reconstruction algorithms employed.

\section{Particle-matter interactions}

Free, energetic particles interact with matter in a variety of ways. The most common interactions are ionization, bremsstrahlung, pair production, and nuclear interactions. These interactions are used to determine the particle's state and properties, but will also limit the performance of a detector. For this reason, the material amount in a detector is a critical parameter during its design as it affects the detector's resolution and efficiency.

Interactions between particles and matter can be categorized into elastic and inelastic interactions.

During elastic interactions, the particle can be deflected but does not lose a significant amount of energy. An example of this is multiple Coulomb scattering, which in first order only affects the direction of the particle's trajectory. Additionally, a shift in the transverse plane relative to the incident direction can build up if the multiple scattering evolves over a sufficiently long distance. This is discussed in more detail in Chapter \ref{ch:dev/dense}.

For inelastic interactions, the particle loses a significant amount of energy or is absorbed in the material. This can happen through ionization, bremsstrahlung, or hadronic interactions. Bremsstrahlung is especially relevant for light particles like electrons. Hadronic interactions are relevant for hadronic particles and lead to the disintegration of the incoming particle. Inelastic interactions lead to the production of secondary particles, which may also be subject to detection. At the same time, they can reduce the number of produced hits in the detector for a given particle and thus reduce the reconstruction efficiency of primary particles. The production of secondaries can increase hit confusion effects and further reduce the efficiency.

\subsection{Detection of charged particles}

Detecting charged particles is the first step in reconstructing their trajectories. Generally, they are detected by measuring some form of interaction with matter. As an example, charged particles typically ionize the surrounding material while traversing it. Particle detectors make use of this by converting the resulting charge into a measurable signal.

Nowadays, semiconducting particle detectors are a very common electronic detection approach. These sensors can be segmented in one dimension, forming strips, or in two dimensions, forming pixels. The measured signal for individual segments can then be associated with a location on the sensor.

\section{Equations of motion}
\label{sec:into/reco/equations-of-motion}

The equations of motion describe the trajectory of a free, charged particle in the presence of a magnetic field. Ignoring any material interactions, the equations can be written as:
\begin{align}
    \label{eq:intro/reco/eom}
    \frac{d^2\mathbf{r}}{ds^2} &= \lambda \mathbf{T} \times \mathbf{B}(\mathbf{r}) \\
    \frac{dt}{ds} &= \sqrt{1 + \frac{m^2}{p^2}}
\end{align}
with the curvature parameter $\lambda = q / p$, the normalized tangent vector $\mathbf{T} =\frac{d\mathbf{r}}{ds}$, magnetic field $\mathbf{B}$, the cartesian position $\mathbf{r}$, the rest frame time $t$, the path length $s$, the absolute momentum $p$, the mass $m$, and the charge $q$.

In a homogeneous magnetic field, the equations can be solved analytically and the particle's trajectory is a helix. In reality, the magnetic field is not homogeneous, and the equations of motion are solved numerically. The most common method to solve the equations of motion is the Runge-Kutta-Nyström method, which is discussed in the next section.

This equation can be extended to include the effects of continuous energy loss which is described in Section \ref{sec:dev/dense/equations-of-motion}.

\section{Runge-Kutta-Nyström method}
\label{sec:into/reco/runge-kutta-nystrom}

The Runge-Kutta-Nyström (RKN) method allows to solve second-order ordinary differential equations (ODE) numerically. It is a specialized Runge-Kutta (RK) method which operates on second-order instead of first-order ODE.\footnote{The RKN method can be derived from the RK method by rewriting the second-order ODE into a first-order ODE.} In track reconstruction, the RKN method is used to solve the equations of motion of a charged particle in a magnetic field, provided in Equation \ref{eq:intro/reco/eom}.

A fourth-order RKN only requires three magnetic field evaluations per step. At the same time, it has proven to be stable and accurate for tracking applications \cite{pub-2009-001}. The integration is performed as follows:
\begin{align}
    \mathbf{T}_{n+1} &= \mathbf{T}_n + \frac{h}{6} (\mathbf{k}_1 + 2 \mathbf{k}_2 + 2 \mathbf{k}_3 + \mathbf{k}_4) \\
    \mathbf{r}_{n+1} &= \mathbf{r}_n + h \mathbf{T}_n + \frac{h^2}{6} (\mathbf{k}_1 + \mathbf{k}_2 + \mathbf{k}_3)
    \label{eq:intro/reco/rkn}
\end{align}
with the next direction estimate $\mathbf{T}_{n+1}$, the next position estimate $\mathbf{r}_{n+1}$, the step size $h$, and the four intermediate RKN steps $\mathbf{k}_i$.

The intermediate steps $\mathbf{k}_i$ are defined as:
\begin{align}
    \mathbf{k}_1 &= \mathbf{f}\left( t_n, \mathbf{y}_n, \mathbf{y}^{\prime}_n \right) \\
    \mathbf{k}_2 &= \mathbf{f}\left( t_n + \frac{h}{2}, \mathbf{y}_n + \frac{h}{2} \mathbf{y}^{\prime}_n + \frac{h^2}{8} \mathbf{k}_1, \mathbf{y}^{\prime}_n + \frac{h}{2} \mathbf{k}_1 \right) \\
    \mathbf{k}_3 &= \mathbf{f}\left( t_n + \frac{h}{2}, \mathbf{y}_n + \frac{h}{2} \mathbf{y}^{\prime}_n + \frac{h^2}{8} \mathbf{k}_1, \mathbf{y}^{\prime}_n + \frac{h}{2} \mathbf{k}_2 \right) \\
    \mathbf{k}_4 &= \mathbf{f}\left( t_n + h, \mathbf{y}_n + h \mathbf{y}^{\prime}_n + \frac{h^2}{2} \mathbf{k}_3, \mathbf{y}^{\prime}_n + h \mathbf{k}_3 \right)
    \label{eq:intro/reco/rkn-steps}
\end{align}
with $\mathbf{y}_n = \left( \mathbf{r}^\mathrm{T} \, t \right)^\mathrm{T}$, $\mathbf{y}^{\prime}_n = \left( \mathbf{T}^\mathrm{T} \, \frac{dt}{ds} \right)^\mathrm{T}$, and the current path length $t_n = s$. The differential equation $\mathbf{f}$ is given by the equations of motion, provided in Equation \ref{eq:intro/reco/eom}.

The global error of the fourth-order RKN method is of order $\mathcal{O}(h^4)$, while the local error is of order $\mathcal{O}(h^5)$. An adaptive step size can be used to improve the performance of the RKN method, which is further discussed in Section \ref{sec:dev/stepper/adaptive-step-size}.

\section{Track parameters}

Track parameters summarize the observable state of a particle's trajectory at a given point. Usually this includes: the position, the local direction, and the local curvature of the trajectory. Time can also be included as a parameter if it is available.

Track parameters can be bound to a surface or be free. Bound track parameters are defined on a surface, while free track parameters are defined in the global 3D coordinate system. The motivation for bound track parameters is that they represent an observable state for tracking detectors.

Track parameterization is ultimately a choice. There is no single best parameterization for all applications. Experiments will use a parameterization that is best suited for their specific needs. Collider experiments are usually symmetric with respect to the beamline and use spherical and/or cylindrical coordinate systems to leverage that. If the magnetic field aligns with the beam axis, curvature only affects the transverse plane (also called bending plane), which can be reflected in the parameterization.

The choice of track parameters in ACTS is discussed in Section \ref{sec:intro/acts/track-parameterization}.

\section{Jacobian and covariance transport}
\label{sec:intro/reco/jac-transport}

In tracking, covariance transport is the process of propagating track parameter uncertainties along the trajectory. It is crucial for some reconstruction algorithms like the Kalman filter based track fitting. Apart from that, the track parameter covariance can be used for physics and validation purposes.

Covariance transport can be achieved by linearizing the track parameter transport into a Jacobian matrix. The Jacobian is a matrix of partial derivatives which describes how track parameters change at a given point with respect to small changes of the initial parameters. The free transport Jacobian matrix can be extracted from the RKN method by taking the derivative of the track parameters with respect to the initial parameters. This is a semi-analytical approach as it depends on the formalism of the numerical integration. In order to get the full transport Jacobian matrix for bound to bound parameter propagation, the Jacobian matrix has to be multiplied with the Jacobian matrix of the bound to free and free to bound transformations.
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{J}_\text{bound\textrightarrow bound} = \mathbf{J}_\text{bound\textrightarrow free} \ \left[ \mathbf{I} + \dv{\mathbf{x}_\text{free}}{s} \ \pdv{s}{\mathbf{x}_\text{free}} \right] \ \mathbf{J}_\text{free\textrightarrow free} \ \mathbf{J}_\text{free\textrightarrow bound}
\end{equation}
The additional term $\dv{\mathbf{x}_\text{free}}{s} \ \pdv{s}{\mathbf{x}_\text{free}}$ is included to deal with path length uncertainties. This can be thought of as a correction term due to projection effects. If the trajectory does not cross the surface perpendicular, the error of the local positions need to be increased. $\dv{\mathbf{x}_\text{free}}{s}$ is the derivative of the free track parameters with respect to the path length $s$ and is given by the equations of motion, provided in Equation \ref{eq:intro/reco/eom}. The fourth-order RKN method evaluates this with $\mathbf{k}_4$. $\pdv{s}{\mathbf{x}_\text{free}}$ is the partial derivative of the path length with respect to the free track parameters and is obtained from the approached surface.

The Jacobian matrix can then be used to transport the covariance matrix of the bound track parameters from one surface to the next. This is done by multiplying the Jacobian matrix with the covariance matrix.
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{\Sigma}_{\text{final}} = \mathbf{J}_{\text{bound\textrightarrow bound}} \ \mathbf{\Sigma}_{\text{initial}} \ \mathbf{J}_{\text{bound\textrightarrow bound}}^\mathrm{T}
\end{equation}
with the covariance on the previous surface $\mathbf{\Sigma}_{\text{initial}}$, and the covariance on the next surface $\mathbf{\Sigma}_{\text{final}}$.

Derivations and validations of the Jacobian and covariance transport can be found in \textcite{bomki-jac}.

\section{Reconstruction chains and objects}

A reconstruction chain is a procedure of steps to ultimately extract all the information of interest from an event. In the context of this thesis, reconstruction chains produce tracks and vertices from primary interactions as final objects. In a broader context of a particle physics experiment, reconstruction chains will also produce higher level objects like jets, missing transverse energy (MET), etc.

Reconstruction objects are the inputs to and outputs from reconstruction steps. They are captured and described with an event data model (EDM). Examples are clusters, spacepoints, seeds, tracks, and vertices. Where clusters are produced from raw sensor data using clustering algorithms, spacepoints are produced from clusters using spacepoint formation algorithms, seeds are produced from spacepoints using pattern recognition algorithms, tracks are produced from seeds using track finding algorithms, and vertices are produced from tracks using vertexing algorithms.

\section{Track and primary vertex reconstruction steps and algorithms}
\label{sec:intro/reco/reco-steps}

Reconstruction steps are performed by one or more reconstruction algorithms and form the building blocks of reconstruction chains. They process input data to produce higher level objects. Different algorithms exist for each reconstruction step. The choice of algorithm depends on the specific requirements of the experiment and the detector design. In this section, common reconstruction steps and algorithms for hadron collider experiments, like ATLAS, are discussed.

\subsection{Clustering}

% there would be more to say about cluster uncertainty, calibration, lorenz angle, pileup dependency, ...

Clustering is usually the very first step in the reconstruction chain. It is the process of grouping raw sensor readings which seem to have been produced by a single particle. This is important for pixel and strip detectors, since a traversing particle may not activate a single sensor cell but rather a cluster of cells, which is illustrated in Figure \ref{fig:intro/reco/clustering}. Clustering is usually done with a connected component labeling (CCL) algorithm.

\begin{figure}[h]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.7\textwidth]{parts/1_introduction/02_reconstruction-introduction/figures/clustering.pdf}
    \caption{Illustration of a particle traversing a pixel sensor in 3D and 2D. Gracefully taken from \textcite{gessinger-thesis}.}
    \label{fig:intro/reco/clustering}
\end{figure}

After a cluster is identified, it is transformed into a measurement. This can be done by equally weighting the sensor cell positions (also called \textit{digital} clustering) or with a weighted average (also called \textit{analog} clustering) based on the time-over-threshold (ToT) or charge amount provided by the sensor. If available, the latter is usually preferred as it provides a better estimate of the hit position.

Clusters can be degraded by two effects: cluster splitting and cluster merging. Cluster splitting occurs when a single hit is reconstructed as multiple clusters. This can happen randomly when sensor cells are below threshold or sensor cells are damaged. Cluster merging occurs when multiple hits are reconstructed as a single cluster. This can happen when the hits are too close, noise activates intermediate sensor cells, or secondary particles are produced. Both effects can lead to a loss of efficiency and resolution.

\subsection{Spacepoint formation}

% there would be more to say about strip spacepoints like ambiguity

Spacepoints form the basis of pattern recognition as they represent the position of all potential hits particles left in the detector in one event. They are produced from measurements by transforming them from their local coordinate system into the global 3D coordinate system. This is done by applying the transformation matrix of the sensor to the measurements. The transformation matrix is obtained from the detector geometry.

For pixel sensors, the spacepoint formation is straightforward as the measurement is 2D and the transformation results in a unique 3D point. For strip sensors the spacepoint formation is more complex as the measurement is 1D and the transformation results in a line in 3D. Strip spacepoints can still be formed in case of two-sided sensors with a stereo angle between them by combining clusters from both sides.

\subsection{Pattern recognition}

% there would be more to say about the algorithms

Pattern recognition is the process of identifying hits that are caused by the same particle and to group them into a track candidate, also called seed. This is usually done with the input of spacepoints. Figure \ref{fig:intro/reco/seeding} illustrates the pattern recognition.

\begin{figure}[h]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.4\textwidth]{parts/1_introduction/02_reconstruction-introduction/figures/seeding.pdf}
    \caption{Illustration of a pattern recognition in the transverse plane on four layers, with particle trajectories as dashed lines, dots as hits, and encircled hits a reconstructed seeds. Gracefully taken from \textcite{gessinger-thesis}.}
    \label{fig:intro/reco/seeding}
\end{figure}

Examples of pattern recognition algorithms are the Hough transform and classical triplet seeding. The Hough transform is a global method which uses all spacepoints to find track candidates simultaneously. It is based on the idea that a spacepoint can be transformed into a line and these lines intersect at a common point if they are created by the same particle. The Hough transform is computationally expensive and not suitable for high multiplicity and high event rates. Classical triplet seeding is a local method which partitions spacepoints into detector regions. It then uses a combinatorial approach to find triplets of spacepoints that are compatible with a track originating from the interaction region. Various cuts can be applied to reduce the combinatorics and to filter out random combinations. The usage of 3 spacepoints is motivated by the fact that 3 points are needed to estimate the track parameters. More than 3 spacepoints can be used to reduce random combinations further. The triplet seeding is fast and can be used for high multiplicity and high event rates.

\subsection{Track finding}

In a classical track reconstruction chain, track finding depends on a pattern recognition step which produces seeds. Seeds are track candidates which only contain a subset of measurements that belong to a single particle. Track finding is the process of finding the complete track from the track candidate seeds. A common algorithm to achieve this is the combinatorial Kalman filter (CKF). The CKF traverses the detector, starting from the seed, and tries to find all the compatible measurements. It uses the Kalman formalism to update the track parameters with encountered measurements. This is illustrated in Figure \ref{fig:intro/reco/finding}. Usually the input seeds contain a number of duplicates and fakes. The CKF has more discrimination power as it is supposed find all measurements and cuts can be applied to filter out fakes. Usually, duplicates have to be filtered out in a subsequent step.

\begin{figure}[h]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.6\textwidth]{parts/1_introduction/02_reconstruction-introduction/figures/finding.pdf}
    \caption{Illustration of how the CKF iteratively explores measurements from a seed outwards. The true trajectory and measurements are shown in green, other combinations are shown with lines between unrelated measurements in orange. Gracefully taken from \textcite{gessinger-thesis}.}
    \label{fig:intro/reco/finding}
\end{figure}

Modern machine learning based algorithms exist which combine pattern recognition and track finding into a single step \cite{exatrkx-2021}\cite{pubnote-gnn}. These algorithms use deep learning techniques to learn the patterns of hits that are caused by a single particle. They can be trained on simulated data and then used to find tracks in real data.

\subsection{Track ambiguity resolution}

Track finding can produce multiple tracks that correspond to the same particle (called \textit{duplicate}) or tracks that do not correspond to one single particle (called \textit{fake}). This is called track ambiguity and has to be treated to reduce systematic biases.

One handle on track ambiguity is the number of shared measurements between tracks. If two tracks share a large number of measurements, they are likely to be duplicates. If a track shares a large number of measurements with multiple other tracks, it is likely to be a fake. This heuristic can be used to filter out duplicates and fakes.

\subsection{Track fitting}

After all measurements that belong to a track have been identified, the track can be fitted to these measurements and the impact parameters can be estimated. In case of track finding with a CKF, this is already done simultaneously with the finding. Different algorithms exist to fit tracks to measurements. The most common one is the Kalman filter (KF) \cite{fruehwirth}, which is a recursive algorithm that uses one measurement at a time to update the local track parameters. The KF is optimal for linear systems and gaussian white noise. This is not the case for electrons, which are subject to highly non-gaussian bremsstrahlung. In this case a Gaussian-sum filter (GSF) \cite{fruehwirth-gsf} can be used, which can be thought of as an extension of the KF.

Alternatively to the KF, a global fit can be used. This can be done by minimizing the $\chi^2$ of the reconstructed trajectory with respect to all measurements. The global fit is not recursive and requires all measurements to be available at once. It is usually slower than the KF but can be more accurate in some cases.

\subsubsection{Kalman filter}

The Kalman filter (KF) is an iterative algorithm that estimates the state of a linear dynamic system from a series of measurements with gaussian white noise. The KF consists of two steps: prediction and update. In the prediction step, the state and its uncertainty are extrapolated based on the previous state and the model of the system. In the update step, the predicted state is updated with a given measurement and its uncertainty. This is illustrated in Figure \ref{fig:intro/reco/kalman}. It is widely used in tracking applications, as it can be used to reject outliers and estimate track parameters and their uncertainties.

\begin{figure}[h]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.6\textwidth]{parts/1_introduction/02_reconstruction-introduction/figures/kalman.pdf}
    \caption{Illustration of the KF forming a filtered state (green) based on measurements (orange) and prediction (blue). Gracefully taken from \textcite{gessinger-thesis}.}
    \label{fig:intro/reco/kalman}
\end{figure}

A powerful feature of the KF is that the state does not need to be directly observable, but can be \textit{hidden}. In tracking, usually only the local position on a sensor is observable, while direction and curvature cannot be directly observed. The KF can be used to estimate these parameters even though they are locally hidden.

The evolution of the state vector is described by
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{x}_{k} = \mathbf{F}_{k-1} \mathbf{x}_{k-1} + \mathbf{w}_{k-1}
\end{equation}
with the state vector $\mathbf{x}_k$, the state transition matrix $\mathbf{F}_k$, and the process noise $\mathbf{w}_k$. The process noise is assumed to be gaussian with zero mean and a covariance matrix $\mathbf{Q}_k$.

For tracking purposes, the state transition matrix $\mathbf{F}_k$ can be obtained by the Jacobian transport matrix from the surface to surface propagation, as described in Section \ref{sec:intro/reco/jac-transport}.

A measurement at step $k$ is described by
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{m}_k = \mathbf{H}_k \mathbf{x}_k + \mathbf{\epsilon}_k
\end{equation}
with the measurement vector $\mathbf{m}_k$, the measurement matrix $\mathbf{H}_k$, and the measurement noise $\mathbf{\epsilon}_k$. The measurement noise is also assumed to be gaussian with zero mean and a covariance matrix $\mathbf{R}_k$.

The KF evolves through two steps: predition and update. In the prediction step, the state and its covariance are extrapolated based on the previous state and the model of the system:
\begin{align}
    \mathbf{x}_{k|k-1} &= \mathbf{F}_{k-1} \mathbf{x}_{k-1|k-1} \\
    \mathbf{C}_{k|k-1} &= \mathbf{F}_{k-1} \mathbf{C}_{k-1|k-1} \mathbf{F}_{k-1}^\mathrm{T} + \mathbf{Q}_{k-1}
\end{align}
An updated state is formed by combining the predicted state and the measurement:
\begin{align}
    \mathbf{x}_{k|k} &= \mathbf{x}_{k|k-1} + \mathbf{K}_k \left( \mathbf{m}_k - \mathbf{H}_k \mathbf{x}_{k|k-1} \right) \\
    \mathbf{C}_{k|k} &= \left( \mathbf{I} - \mathbf{K}_k \mathbf{H}_k \right) \mathbf{C}_{k|k-1} \\
    \mathbf{K}_k &= \mathbf{C}_{k|k-1} \mathbf{H}_k^\mathrm{T} \left( \mathbf{R}_k + \mathbf{H}_k \mathbf{C}_{k|k-1} \mathbf{H}_k^\mathrm{T} \right)^{-1}
\end{align}
with the optimal Kalman gain $\mathbf{K}_k$, the predicted state $\mathbf{x}_{k|k-1}$, the predicted covariance $\mathbf{C}_{k|k-1}$, the updated state $\mathbf{x}_{k|k}$, and the updated covariance $\mathbf{C}_{k|k}$.

One should note that the filtered covariance matrix $\mathbf{C}_{k|k}$ is always smaller than the predicted covariance matrix $\mathbf{C}_{k|k-1}$. For this reason, the initial covariance matrix $\mathbf{C}_0$ has to be inflated to avoid biasing the filter and to estimate the final uncertainties just from the measurement and process noise.

The final filtered state and covariance are optimal as they contain information from all the available measurements. For tracking purposes it is important to obtain optimal parameters at the interaction point, and sometimes even at intermediate states. This can be achieved with a smoother, or by running the KF a second time in the reverse direction. A common smoother is the Rauch-Tung-Striebel (RTS) smoother, which is a fixed-interval smoother and operates in state space dimensions.

A $\chi^2$ value can be computed between measurements and predicted, filtered, or smoothed states:
\begin{align}
    \mathbf{r}_k &= \mathbf{m}_k - \mathbf{H}_k \mathbf{x}_k \\
    \chi^2_k &= \mathbf{r}_k^\mathrm{T} \mathbf{S}_k^{-1} \mathbf{r}_k
\end{align}
with the appropriate state vector $\mathbf{x}_k$, the residual $\mathbf{r}_k$, and the residual covariance $\mathbf{S}_k$.

For predicted states, the residual covariance is given by
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{S}_{k|k-1} = \mathbf{R}_k + \mathbf{H}_k \mathbf{C}_{k|k-1} \mathbf{H}_k^\mathrm{T} \ \text{.}
\end{equation}
While for filtered and smoothed states the residual covariance is given by
\begin{align}
    \mathbf{S}_{k|k} &= \mathbf{R}_k - \mathbf{H}_k \mathbf{C}_{k|k} \mathbf{H}_k^\mathrm{T} \ \text{and} \\
    \mathbf{S}_{k|n} &= \mathbf{R}_k - \mathbf{H}_k \mathbf{C}_{k|n} \mathbf{H}_k^\mathrm{T} \ \text{.}
\end{align}
The filtered and smoothed residual covariances are smaller than the predicted residual covariance as they include more information and they are correlated with the measurement.

The $\chi^2$ value can be used to select measurements and filter outliers.

\subsection{Primary vertexing}

\begin{figure}[h]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.6\textwidth]{parts/1_introduction/02_reconstruction-introduction/figures/vertexing.pdf}
    \caption{Illustration of a set of three vertices in a proton-proton collision, distinguishing between primary hard-scatter, primary pileup, and secondary vertices. Gracefully taken from \textcite{gessinger-thesis}.}
    \label{fig:intro/reco/vertexing}
\end{figure}

Vertexing is the process of estimating the point where particles from a single interactions were produced. In case of primary vertexing, the reconstruction of vertices from the primary collisions. This is usually done with the impact parameters of fitted tracks. Figure \ref{fig:intro/reco/vertexing} illustrates the difference between primary vertices and secondary vertices.

Similar to tracking, vertexing is usually done in a sequence of steps: seeding, finding, and fitting.

Vertex seeding identifies regions of interest, usually based on track density. For primary vertexing this can be done along the beamline using a clustering algorithm or by finding peaks in the track density. The track density can be expressed analytically as a sum of gaussians, where mean and width come from the track parameters and their uncertainty \cite{pubnote-2019-015}. Peaks can then be found numerically by gradient decent or by binning the track density and finding local maxima directly.

Vertex finding proceeds by using seeds to identify nearby tracks that are compatible with the vertex seed. This process is typically carried out iteratively, evaluating one seed at a time, which imparts a greedy character to the algorithm. The selected tracks are subsequently passed to a vertex fitter.

The vertex fitting then uses the selected tracks to fit the vertex position. Simultaneously, the track parameters can be fitted to the vertex, improving the impact parameter resolution further. Common vertex fitters are Kalman-based fitters \cite{fruehwirth} and the Billoir fitter \cite{billoir}.
